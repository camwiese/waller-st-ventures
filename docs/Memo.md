**The Market Is Much Bigger Than It Looks**

The current market for corneal endothelial treatments via transplant is roughly $500 million per year, but that number only reflects the patients who are actually being treated today, which is only a small fraction of those who need it. The existing system is so constrained by donor supply, surgical complexity, geographic limitations, and cost that only the most severe cases in the most developed markets ever reach the operating table.

When a frozen dose can be shipped anywhere in the world for a fraction of the current cost and a surgeon can administer it in minutes rather than hours, demand expands dramatically; you're not just serving existing patients more efficiently, you're reaching millions of people who were never in the market to begin with.

The true addressable market is closer to $10 billion when you account for this latent demand, grounded in data showing that roughly 4% of adults over 40 have some form of corneal endothelial disease, the vast majority of whom are not being treated today. **The Risks**

This is early-stage biotech with a long road ahead and real risks, and I want to be straightforward about that.

The biggest unknown is whether cryopreserved cells will perform as well in human patients as they have in the lab. This is the central question the first-in-human trial is designed to answer, and it is not a binary pass/fail situation because outcomes exist on a spectrum. If cryopreserved cells demonstrate equivalent or near-equivalent efficacy to fresh cells, PST is positioned for a competitive acquisition process. If efficacy is moderately lower but still clinically meaningful, the team may need to iterate on the formulation or pursue a different regulatory pathway, extending the timeline and likely requiring additional capital. If the approach doesn't translate to humans at all, that represents a more fundamental setback, though even in that scenario the team's proprietary biomarker discoveries and manufacturing IP may retain value.

Beyond efficacy, the regulatory timeline could shift and the competitive landscape could evolve in ways that are difficult to predict, though it's worth noting that cryopreservation of corneal endothelial cells is an extraordinarily specialized field with very few credible teams working in it.
